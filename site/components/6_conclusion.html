5. Conclusion

A geometric blind-prediction residual test for potential-dependent temporal propagation has been applied to SN Refsdal, the only lensed supernova with five resolved images and precision time delays. The key results are:

  1. Under GR, all five algebraic loop sums are identically zero by construction. The genuine TEP observable is a blind-prediction residual — the discrepancy between observed delays and GR model predictions — not a closure violation.
  2. The inner Einstein cross is a null region where the $\mu \rightarrow \kappa$ proxy fails (shear degeneracy; rank-order agreement $P \approx 3$%). All probative content comes from the S4–SX contrast.
  3. The two SX loops yield predicted residuals of $-8.5$ d (SNR = 66) and $-14.5$ d (SNR = 63), far exceeding the 5.6 d measurement uncertainty. Signal-energy analysis confirms $D_{\rm eff} \approx 2.0$.
  4. The loop sum is insensitive to the Mass Sheet Degeneracy (a uniform convergence sheet rescales all delays by the same factor), but the empirical residual remains lens-model-limited.
  5. The pre-specified blind-only Wilcoxon signed-rank test gives $p = 0.016$ ($2.2\sigma$); the all-eight-model test gives $p = 0.0078$ ($2.4\sigma$). Robustness checks (LOO, microlensing MC, hierarchical Bayes with GR-centred prior) preserve the directional pattern. Bayesian model comparison is non-decisive (BF $\approx 1$), consistent with the current precision regime.
  6. Internal consistency check — $H_0$. The proxy-model-corrected SN Refsdal $H_0$ shifts from 66.6 to 69.3 km s$^{-1}$ Mpc$^{-1}$ — definitional, not independent, since $\alpha_{\rm lens}$ was calibrated from the same data.
  7. If independent delay measurements yield $|\mathcal{R}_{\rm obs}(\mathrm{S1, S4, SX})| < 1$ d, the log-magnification ansatz is falsified. Conversely, a residual consistent with $-14.5$ d would constitute direct geometric evidence once lens-model uncertainties are reduced to $\sigma < 5$ d.
  8. SN 2025wny ($z_s = 2.011$) provides the next falsifiable target: predicted GR discrepancies of 1–15 d at $\alpha_{\rm lens} \approx -0.055$, with pre-registered thresholds (§4.9).

The current evidence is suggestive but not decisive. The limiting factor is lens-model precision ($\pm$16–60 d), not measurement noise ($\pm$5.6 d) or signal amplitude (14.5 d). Reducing model uncertainties to $\sigma < 5$ d — achievable with extended-source modelling and next-generation mass reconstructions — would push this test to $>5\sigma$. This is a well-defined, falsifiable, purely geometric test whose power scales with model precision: the path from suggestive to decisive is entirely observational.
