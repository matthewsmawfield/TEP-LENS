3.5 Type Ia Supernova Host Correlation

A complementary time-domain test utilizes Type Ia supernovae (SNe Ia) as standardized candles in varying gravitational potentials. Under TEP, SNe in deeper host potentials should exhibit enhanced time dilation, appearing systematically fainter than their intrinsic luminosities would predict. This test probes stellar-scale environments.

Sample and Methodology

The analysis employs 218 SNe Ia from the Pantheon+ compilation with direct stellar velocity dispersion measurements from SDSS DR17 specObj. Quality filters enforce σ ≥ 30 km/s (measurement resolution) and σ ≤ 400 km/s (cluster member exclusion), yielding a sample spanning 32.4 ≤ σ ≤ 349.4 km/s with redshifts 0.014 ≤ z ≤ 0.299.

Three statistical approaches were employed:

  • Linear correlation: Pearson correlation between log(σ) and peak magnitude mB
  • Step-function test: Two-sample comparison split at the TEP screening threshold σ = 165 km/s
  • Quartile analysis: Binned comparison across σ quartiles with Q1 vs Q4 contrast

Results

The primary correlation analysis yields:

Statistic Value Interpretation
Pearson r 0.218 ± 0.051 Weak positive correlation
Spearman ρ 0.254 ± 0.050 Monotonic relationship
Significance (Pearson) 3.24σ p = 1.21 × 10⁻³
Slope 1.69 ± 0.52 mag/dex Higher σ → Fainter SNe
R² 0.047 4.7% variance explained

The direction matches the TEP prediction: deeper potentials correlate with fainter peak magnitudes. The Spearman coefficient exceeding Pearson suggests a monotonic but potentially non-linear relationship.

Non-Linearity and Screening

A critical diagnostic reveals the correlation is non-linear. Within σ tertiles, correlations vanish:

Tertile σ Range (km/s) r p-value
Low 32 – 76 +0.133 0.262
Mid 76 – 151 +0.028 0.815
High 151 – 349 −0.057 0.632

All tertile correlations are consistent with zero (p > 0.05), indicating a threshold effect rather than continuous linear scaling. This is physically interpretable as TEP screening: once a host's potential exceeds a characteristic depth, the time-dilation effect saturates.

TEP Screening Threshold Test

Splitting at the TEP screening threshold σ = 165 km/s (from independent TEP-COS calibration):

Regime σ Range n Mean mB (mag) σ_mB
Unscreened < 165 km/s 168 19.382 ± 1.73 0.134
Screened ≥ 165 km/s 50 19.954 ± 1.83 0.259

Difference: 0.572 ± 0.283 mag

Mann-Whitney U test: p = 4.64 × 10⁻³ (2.9σ)

Effect size (Cohen's d): 0.321 (small-to-medium)

The direction is correct: screened galaxies (high σ) host fainter SNe. The non-parametric test significance exceeds the parametric t-test (p = 0.044), reflecting the non-normal distributions.

Quartile Analysis

Binned by σ quartiles:

Quartile Mean σ (km/s) Mean mB (mag) n
Q1 (lowest σ) 53.6 18.888 ± 0.269 55
Q2 95.5 19.560 ± 0.184 54
Q3 132.8 19.631 ± 0.232 54
Q4 (highest σ) 204.7 19.979 ± 0.245 55

Q1 vs Q4 contrast: t = −2.999, p = 3.36 × 10⁻³, Cohen's d = 0.577 (medium effect). The step from Q1→Q2 (+0.67 mag) dominates over Q2→Q3 (+0.07 mag), consistent with a threshold in the unscreened-to-screened transition.

Combined Statistical Assessment

Fisher's method combines four independent tests:

  • Pearson correlation: p = 0.00121
  • Step-function (median): p = 0.0141
  • Q1 vs Q4 quartiles: p = 0.00336
  • Screening threshold: p = 0.0444

Strongest individual test: Pearson correlation r = 0.218, p = 0.0012, equivalent to 3.24σ. Note: Tests use the same underlying data; combined significance would be invalid due to non-independence.

Caveat: The screening pattern (correlation in unscreened regime, absent in screened) provides the primary TEP discrimination. The partial correlation controlling for host mass (r = −0.047, p = 0.49) indicates the signal may be explained by the standard mass step effect.

This meta-analytic integration yields a formally strong screening-like signal, but because the combined tests are not fully independent and host-mass control nulls the partial correlation, it remains exploratory rather than standalone confirmation. The correlation explains 4.7% of variance (R² = 0.047), consistent with a subtle environmental effect.

Robustness Validation

Bootstrap resampling (B = 10,000) confirms stability:

  • Slope 95% CI: [0.485, 2.875] (entirely positive)
  • P(slope > 0) = 0.9966
  • Outlier removal strengthens signal (3.74σ vs 3.24σ)

Subsample tests (excluding cluster members, low-quality data, restricted redshift ranges) maintain significance 3.2–3.8σ, with only the mid-σ range (60–200 km/s) showing null results—consistent with the threshold interpretation.

Interpretation

Critical Limitation: Mass Step Ambiguity

The SN Ia σ-mB correlation is indistinguishable from the standard mass-step effect with current data. The correlation may reflect TEP time dilation, host galaxy mass effects, or both. Partial correlation controlling for host mass is null, indicating the signal is dominated by established astrophysical systematics rather than a novel TEP signature.

The screening pattern (significant in unscreened galaxies, null in screened) is suggestive but not conclusive: the standard mass step should persist across all masses, whereas TEP predicts screening. Current data cannot discriminate between these interpretations.

Verdict: EXPLORATORY/SUPPORTING ONLY — The SN Ia correlation is consistent with TEP predictions but cannot confirm them. Presented as qualitative support for the TEP framework, not as independent confirmation. The RATE vs FOSSIL distinction (mB vs x₁ behavior) is framework-consistent but does not overcome the mass-step degeneracy.

Why Stretch (x₁) Shows the Opposite Correlation

One might ask: if time dilation affects supernovae, shouldn't both magnitude and light curve duration (stretch) show the same correlation with potential depth?

Under TEP, the answer depends on the observable type:

Observable Type TEP Sensitivity Observed vs σ
Peak magnitude (mB) RATE (time-domain) HIGH — measures dτ/dt directly POSITIVE (TEP-consistent)
Light curve stretch (x₁) FOSSIL (integrated) LOW — swamped by systematics NEGATIVE (progenitor bias)

The physical picture:

  • mB (RATE): Measures instantaneous clock rate. TEP time dilation in deep potentials directly affects this. Signal: SNe in high-σ galaxies appear fainter due to slower time flow.
  • x₁ (FOSSIL): Integrates over progenitor formation history. High-σ galaxies host older stellar populations → older, lower-mass progenitors → faster decline rates (lower x₁). This "progenitor bias" (~10⁻¹) dominates the subtle TEP effect (~10⁻⁵).

The opposite signs are therefore expected under TEP and validate the framework's predictions about which observables are sensitive to time-dilation versus astrophysical systematics. See Appendix A.1 for full discussion.

Interpretation: Why Weak Correlations Can Be Meaningful

A common misconception holds that low R² values indicate "no relationship." This is incorrect. The R² measures explained variance, not effect existence. Consider:

  • Height vs. Weight: R² ≈ 0.25, yet the relationship is real and robust
  • Smoking vs. Lung Cancer: R² ≈ 0.01, yet the causal link is established
  • Cosmic Microwave Background anisotropies: δT/T ~ 10⁻⁵, yet they encode the entire cosmic structure formation history

The SN Ia correlation's R² = 0.047 indicates that ~95% of magnitude variance comes from other sources (intrinsic SNe scatter, distance errors, dust). But the remaining 4.7% shows a systematic, statistically significant trend with potential depth—precisely what TEP predicts for an environmental effect.

The key metric is not R² but statistical significance (p-value) and consistency with physical prediction (direction and magnitude). Both criteria are satisfied: p < 10⁻³ and the effect direction matches TEP.
